Here is what we have covered in class so far

01.25.2010: Introduction and Course Overview

Overview of the goals of this course and a brief introduction to some biological concepts that will be used throughout the course. Also, a brief review of some of the CS-related concepts that are required for the course.

Readings

The first two chapters of the text book. These chapters should be treated as reference material, in the sense that they should be understood (not memorized), especially for students with no biology background. Some of the key concepts introduced here will be revisited throughout the course.

Also, I have made available online some additional background material which should be read if the need arises. They make a very nice bed-time reading. Note that even though the reading may look long, there is a lot of duplication among the various reading items.

Additional Material

  • What is Bioinformatics?
  • Molecular Biology for Computer Scientists

02.01.2010: Sequence Comparison and Alignments

Motivation for Sequence Comparison

Focus on the problem of comparing DNA and amino acid sequences in order to primarily detect homology relationships and to a somewhat lesser extent, functional relationships. Establish the connection between sequence-similarity-based homology detection with sequence alignment and introduce the various issues involved in aligning two or more biological sequences.

Reading Material

  1. Sections 4.1-4.4 from text book
  2. Slides

Algorithms for Pairwise Sequence Alignments

Focus on solving the problem of aligning a pair of sequences and cover dynamic programming-based algorithms for global, local, and semi-global sequence alignment. In addition, algorithms to handle the different gap models are discussed and analyzed.

Reading Material

  1. Section 4.4, 5.2 from textbook
  2. Chapter 11 from Gusfield ( Core String Edits, Alignments, and Dynamic Programming)

02.08.2010: Multiple Sequence Alignment Algorithms

Focus on solving the problem of aligning a set of sequences together and cover various topics related to scoring multiple sequence alignments, representing them, and constructing them using both optimal dynamic-programming based algorithms as well as various heuristic approaches. Some of the heuristic approaches are guaranteed to be within a small constant factor from the optimal alignment, whereas some of the other heuristic approaches focus more on computing multiple sequence alignments that are correct in an evolutionary sense.

Reading Material

  1. Chapter 14.1 to 14.6.1 only from Gusfield
  2. Chapters 6.4, 6.5 from textbook
  3. Lecture Slides

02.22.2010: Database Search

Focus on why is database search needed. How could one come up with faster and accurate ways of going through and finding similar sequences. Check out the indexing methods used by Fasta and BLAST.

Reading Material

  2. Section 5.3 from your text book

03.01.2010: Scoring Matrices

Focus on how to derive scoring matrices and provide a probabilistic interpretation for their construction derivation. The overall framework is illustrated by describing how the PAM and BLOSUM matrices have been derived.

Scoring matrices are critical in encoding biological information into the alignment procedures. It is the use of the appropriate scoring matrix that ensures (up to a point) that the highest scoring alignment will actually be able to detect homology by aligning against each other the residues of two proteins that evolved from the same ancestral residue.

Reading Material

  1. Chapter 5.1 from textbook
  2. Lecture Slides

Additional Material

03.01.2010: Statistical Significance of Alignment Scores

Focus on how to assess the statistical significance of alignment scores and present and overview of the methods that are commonly used.

Reading Material

  1. Lecture Slides
  2. Sections 5.4 & 6.3 from the textbook.

03.01.2010: Markov Models

Focus on probabilistic models for sequence classification and annotation and describe Markov Chains and Hidden Markov models, which are the most widely used Markovian models in bioinformatics.

Reading Material

  1. Lecture Slides
  2. Chapter 3 from Durbin
  3. Chapter 6.2 from the textbook

03.22.2010+03.29.2010: Protein Structure Prediction

Discuss the secondary structure organization of protein molecules and different ways of determining them from their primary sequence.

Reading Material

  3. Chapter 13 from the textbook

04.19.2010: Gene Prediction

Focus on the problem of finding the genes in an organisms chromosomal DNA sequence. Describe the various sequence-based signals that exists and the various methods that have been developed for gene prediction in prokaryotic and eukaryotic organisms.

Reading Material
